# If AI replaces workers, should it also pay taxes?

- Score: 524 | [HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=46268709) | Link: https://english.elpais.com/technology/2025-11-30/if-ai-replaces-workers-should-it-also-pay-taxes.html

## TL;DR
AI is driving huge profits and layoffs, raising fears that shrinking wage income will erode tax bases built on payroll and income taxes. The article surveys economists and institutions (IMF, Brookings, Oxford, OECD experts) who mostly oppose a special “AI tax,” citing definitional problems and innovation distortions. They instead propose rebalancing from labor to capital and excess‑profits taxes as corporate rates fall and capital gains rise. HN commenters echo that we should tax owners and outcomes, not “AI,” and worry more about inequality and automation’s endgame than robot-specific levies.

## Comment pulse
- Tax the owners of AI, not “AI” itself; shift burden from labor to capital — counterpoint: heavy capital taxes can slow productivity and wage growth.  
- AI resembles tractors or other machinery: we shouldn’t tax tools, but the larger economy and profits they enable, while cushioning fast technological transitions.  
- Many see “AI tax” proposals as incoherent and easy to evade; the real challenge is long‑run distribution if automation makes labor largely optional.  

## LLM perspective
- Frame policy around labor share of income: automatically raise capital/excess-profits taxes when labor’s macro share falls below targets.  
- Pair any AI-related tax changes with retraining stipends, wage insurance, and portable benefits funded from a broad-based automation dividend.  
- Track sectors where AI measurably replaces headcount; pilot time-limited surcharges on excess profits there, evaluated against employment and investment data.
