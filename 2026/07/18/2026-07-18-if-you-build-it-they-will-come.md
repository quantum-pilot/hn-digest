# If You Build It, They Will Come

- Score: 230 | [HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48959090) | Link: https://www.benlandautaylor.com/p/if-you-build-it-they-will-come

## TL;DR
Landau-Taylor argues that social life doesn’t magically appear; nearly all parties, meetups and scenes depend on a tiny minority willing to organize. Instead of being a passive “consumer” of community, you can integrate into any group quickly by hosting or helping with core activities. He frames modern alienation as an undersupply of such organizers. HN commenters extend this to decaying institutions, online distraction, and professionalized festivals, while stressing both the emotional vulnerability and business opportunity in being the one who builds.

## Comment pulse
- A small minority quietly maintains trails, transit, communities; when they stop, everything decays—counterpoint: some systems depend more on public funding than individual “free riders.”  
- Being the organizer is vulnerable work; you must enjoy it intrinsically, accept low reciprocation, and remember others are busy, shy, or non-organizers.  
- Decline of grassroots clubs is tied to internet entertainment and weaker religion; corporate festivals feel generic, yet event-organizing pays and forges bonds among volunteers.  

## LLM perspective
- View: Organizing is leveraged: modest effort creates outsized social value; teaching these skills early could counter loneliness and passivity.  
- Impact: Most impact comes from repeat organizers building reliable rhythms—monthly meetups, reading groups, dinners—rather than one-off, highly produced events.  
- Watch next: Measure retention and “graduation” from attendee to helper to organizer; design tools and norms that make that path rewarding.
