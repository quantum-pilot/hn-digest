# AI advice made people 3x less accurate but 2x confident, researchers found

- Score: 214 | [HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48971738) | Link: https://thenextweb.com/news/ai-advice-suppresses-critical-thinking-wrong-answers-study

- TL;DR  
  Researchers quizzed people on obscure movie details, sometimes letting them consult a deliberately weak AI. Without AI, 44% said “I don’t know” and 27% were correct; with AI, only 3% admitted ignorance, accuracy fell to 9%, yet confidence jumped to 76%. Small monetary incentives barely curbed this “cognitive surrender.” The article worries about kids and AI products that never say “I don’t know.” HN commenters add anecdotes about degraded online advice, critique the study design, and warn of AI-driven echo chambers.

- Comment pulse  
  - Online advice is degrading → Reddit answers are often unlabelled LLM paste, undermining trust, genuine expertise, and the social, tailored value of asking humans.  
  - Study isn't AI-specific → critics liken it to testing with an error-filled textbook. — counterpoint: defenders say LLMs’ ease-of-use makes such overtrust uniquely risky.  
  - Longer-term fears emerge → commenters expect AI-powered echo chambers, erosion of professionals’ “feel for numbers,” and disasters if overconfident systems replace experts in safety-critical domains.

- LLM perspective  
  - View: Treat LLMs as fallible collaborators; design tasks and prompts that explicitly allow, even reward, “I don’t know” responses.  
  - Impact: Education, Q&A platforms, and workplaces should flag AI-generated answers and train users to verify claims in unfamiliar domains.  
  - Watch next: Experiments with accurate models, real-world tasks, and interfaces that surface uncertainty or abstain instead of confidently guessing.
