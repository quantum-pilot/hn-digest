# GLM 5.2 is nearly as accurate as a human book keeper

- Score: 165 | [HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48850414) | Link: https://toot-books.pages.dev/blog/glm-5-2-vat-benchmark

### TL;DR
GLM 5.2 was benchmarked on a real UK VAT bookkeeping task and reportedly matched human bookkeepers on classifying transactions, given bank feeds, invoices and “user notes.” Commenters stress the benchmark excludes messy but crucial work like chasing documents, interpreting ambiguous situations and detecting fraud. People report promising productivity gains when LLMs handle rote posting under human review, yet remain wary of hallucinations, non-determinism, opaque vendors and unclear legal liability if an AI-driven accounting workflow misstates taxes.

### Comment pulse
- Benchmark narrows task to categorizing known invoices, omitting messy retrieval/judgment, so “near-human” claims overstate automation readiness—counterpoint: paired tools already fetch invoices reasonably well.  
- Accuracy isn’t enough; taxpayers remain legally liable, and LLM hallucinations make non-deterministic accounting and fraud/social-engineering attacks especially scary for regulatory work.  
- Many see value in automating low-level posting while humans review, but worry about opaque startups handling financial data and taking no responsibility to tax authorities.  

### LLM perspective
- View: Bookkeeping looks highly automatable at the classification layer, but real-world deployment hinges on retrieval reliability, controls, and liability allocation.  
- Impact: Short term, small firms can shrink junior accounting roles, keep senior reviewers; vendors that underwrite risk could gain share.  
- Watch next: Track audited benchmarks on larger, noisier datasets plus deterministic guardrails; also regulatory guidance on acceptable AI use in tax preparation.
