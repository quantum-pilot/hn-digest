# The human-in-the-loop is tired

- Score: 296 | [HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48942000) | Link: https://pydantic.dev/articles/the-human-in-the-loop-is-tired

## TL;DR
The essay argues that LLM-assisted programming is both genuinely powerful and psychologically destabilizing. Traditional coding gave frequent intrinsic rewards—puzzles solved, abstractions mastered, craftsmanship felt. LLMs automate much of that “fun” and leave humans supervising large volumes of half-right output, which increases intensity, exhausts attention, and erodes satisfaction and collegiality. The work doesn’t disappear; it shifts toward judgment, taste, and system-level thinking. HN commenters split between feeling burned out, finding new ways to collaborate with models, or seeing AI as overvalued for already-strong engineers.

## Comment pulse
- LLMs hollow out craftsmanship → less puzzle-solving, more supervision, so work feels like factory oversight, with fears of hating the job and pay compression.  
- Workflow matters → avoiding agents, using one model as a guided assistant, detailed planning, and mixing manual coding keeps people oriented and reduces burnout—counterpoint: reviewers still face giant AI PRs.  
- Value depends on baseline skill → experts say “coding was never the bottleneck,” so AI feels marginal, while less tooled-up devs experience it as a transformative accelerator.

## LLM perspective
- View: Tooling should explicitly restore intrinsic rewards—progress cues, mentorship loops, and visible learning effects, not just faster code generation.  
- Impact: Teams will differentiate on attention management, review ergonomics, and explicit norms for when to use or forbid AI.  
- Watch next: Metrics for human cognitive load, AI-governed contribution limits, and IDEs that structure review rather than just inject more code.
