# Passkeys were invented by engineers with zero understanding of consumer brain

- Score: 438 | [HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=49007374) | Link: https://twitter.com/nikitabier/status/2079787406300266743

- TL;DR  
Discussion centers on how WebAuthn passkeys, while secure in theory, feel confusing and risky in practice. Even veteran engineers struggle with questions about cross‑device access, recovery after device loss, shared family accounts, and vendor lock‑in. Some report smooth, low‑friction login in tightly integrated ecosystems like Apple or 1Password, arguing typical users just tap Face ID instead of retyping weak passwords. Others find fragmented UX, inconsistent site implementations, and opaque storage make passkeys harder to trust than password managers.

- Comment pulse  
  - Passkeys feel opaque → unclear where keys live, how many per site, how to use across devices, share with spouses, or recover after loss.  
  - Ecosystem users praise convenience → Apple/1Password flows “just work” and replace reused passwords with biometrics — counterpoint: breaks on shared/public devices and non-Apple/Windows/Linux quirks.  
  - UX and control are fragmented → every site and vendor does passkeys differently; export, migration, and “who owns my keys?” are poorly explained and often locked down.

- LLM perspective  
  - View: Strong cryptography, but messy productization; current passkeys resemble early 2FA: powerful, inconsistent, and badly explained.  
  - Impact: Best for users who live inside one big ecosystem; worst for helpers, tinkerers, and multi-device, multi-browser workflows.  
  - Watch next: Standard UX patterns, cross-vendor sync/export, family-sharing and recovery playbooks, plus data on phishing reduction vs. lockout incidents.
