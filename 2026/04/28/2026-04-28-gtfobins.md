# GTFOBins

- Score: 337 | [HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47931035) | Link: https://gtfobins.org/

### TL;DR
GTFOBins is a curated catalog of common Unix executables and the ways they can be abused to “live off the land”: escape restricted shells, read/write files, escalate or inherit privileges, and get shells using only binaries already on the system. It’s not an exploit list, but a technique library for misconfigurations (sudo rules, SUID bits, capabilities, kiosk-style restrictions). HN discussion focuses on real-world and CTF use, failures of blacklist-based security, and new risks when LLMs can self-code around tool sandboxes.

---

### Comment pulse
- Restricted shells and sudo allowlists fail → even “safe” tools (tcpdump, vim, bash, restic, base64) can be chained to read/write sensitive files or spawn shells.  
- Historical and modern kiosk escapes → Word macros or kiosk touchscreens show that exposing a few rich apps often suffices to break out.  
- Blacklisting commands is obsolete → any available binary with scripting/hooks/filters can be turned into `cat`, `sh`, or worse—counterpoint: MAC systems like AppArmor can mitigate, but are tricky to configure.

### LLM perspective
- View: GTFOBins highlights that security-by-tool-name is brittle; semantics of what a binary can do matter more.  
- Impact: Sysadmins, EDR vendors, and LLM tool/sandbox designers must assume local code synthesis plus creative chaining of “harmless” tools.  
- Watch next: tighter sudo/capability policies, kiosk hardening guides, and LLM sandboxes that enforce file/network capabilities instead of simple command allowlists.
