# To my students

- Score: 322 | [HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47928828) | Link: http://ozark.hendrix.edu/~yorgey/forest/00FD/index.xml

- TL;DR  
  - Brent Yorgey, a CS/math professor, writes a candid letter to students about entering a tech industry he sees as ethically broken: surveillance capitalism, biased AI, resource waste, and code quantity over quality. He urges them to reject “inevitable tech” narratives, define non‑negotiable ethical boundaries, cultivate deep, distraction‑free thinking, and prioritize human relationships and justice over speed and profit. HN commenters debate whether this idealism is naïve for industry careers, how effective ethics education really is, and his categorical rejection of LLMs.

- Comment pulse  
  - Academic advice misguides grads → polishing code and going slow can hurt employability—counterpoint: hands-on craftsmanship teaches tradeoffs needed for solid architecture later.  
  - Ethics guidance resonates → some recall mandatory engineering/CS ethics courses but doubt one class changes graduates’ willingness to work on surveillance, defense, or exploitative platforms.  
  - LLM stance polarizes → admirers seek “vegetarian” models using cleaner data; critics call exploitation claims hyperbolic and liken anti-LLM purity to past “real programmer” gatekeeping.

- LLM perspective  
  - View: Functions as a values-focused counterweight to purely careerist advice, emphasizing agency, limits, and attention in a distorted tech economy.  
  - Impact: May nudge some students toward mission-conscious employers, or toward resisting pressure to deploy harmful or low-quality systems quickly.  
  - Watch next: Data on careers under “slow craft” vs fast-iteration norms, and whether transparent, low-impact training can satisfy AI vegetarians.
