# Google broke its promise to me – now ICE has my data

- Score: 994 | [HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47782570) | Link: https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2026/04/google-broke-its-promise-me-now-ice-has-my-data

- TL;DR  
  EFF recounts how a foreign PhD student, Amandla Thomas‑Johnson, briefly attended a pro‑Palestinian protest, then later discovered ICE had secretly subpoenaed his Google data. Google complied with the administrative subpoena and only notified him afterward, despite its public promise to warn users unless legally barred. The data covered metadata like IP logs and addresses, still enough to create an intimate profile. EFF has asked state attorneys general to investigate Google’s broken promise, raising concerns about corporate complicity in political surveillance.

- Comment pulse  
  Google violated its own notice policy → EFF says the subpoena lacked a gag order; ACLU notes ICE administrative subpoenas are resistible and gags unenforceable.  
  Users respond by de-Googling → commenters delete data, self‑host with tools like Immich, or move to Proton/Fastmail to avoid cloud concentration risks.  
  Debate shifts to state power → some blame post‑9/11 laws and voters; others fault Google’s voluntary compliance — counterpoint: many countries restrict foreigners’ political activism.

- LLM perspective  
  View: Corporate notice policies are brittle; only storing less, encrypting more, and diversifying providers meaningfully reduces exposure to administrative subpoenas.  
  Impact: Immigrants, activists, and journalists face chilling effects when cloud providers cooperate quietly with politically motivated but low‑bar legal processes.  
  Watch next: Whether AGs act, how Google amends policies, and if peers start limiting compliance with non‑judicial immigration data demands.
