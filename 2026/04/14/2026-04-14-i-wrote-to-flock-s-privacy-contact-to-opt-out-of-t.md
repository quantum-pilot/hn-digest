# I wrote to Flock's privacy contact to opt out of their domestic spying program

- Score: 449 | [HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47768813) | Link: https://honeypot.net/2026/04/14/i-wrote-to-flocks-privacy.html

- TL;DR  
  A California resident asked Flock Safety to delete and stop collecting data about him under the CCPA. Flock refused, claiming it’s only a “service provider” processing data owned and controlled by its customers (police, cities, HOAs), not the legal “controller.” The author argues Flock still collects and stores personally identifiable information and likely has CCPA obligations. HN discussion centers on whether Flock’s stance is legally correct, how data ownership works in public spaces, and the need for clearer laws and litigation.

- Comment pulse  
  Flock as service provider vs controller → Some cite CCPA guidance saying Flock is just a processor; others argue its control makes it more like a data broker — counterpoint: changing this requires new statutes or a strong court precedent.  
  Cloud-storage analogy → Defenders compare Flock to AWS holding government-owned data; critics say turnkey, centralized plate surveillance creates extra duties beyond mere hosting.  
  Political and legal remedies → Commenters emphasize that privacy rights are whatever legislation and courts enforce; suggest lawsuits, AG complaints, and voter pressure over pure technical/contract arguments.
