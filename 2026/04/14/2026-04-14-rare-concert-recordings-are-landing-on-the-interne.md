# Rare concert recordings are landing on the Internet Archive

- Score: 488 | [HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47765604) | Link: https://techcrunch.com/2026/04/13/thousands-of-rare-concert-recordings-are-landing-on-the-internet-archive-listen-now/

- TL;DR  
    - Since the 1980s, Chicago fan Aadam Jacobs has stealth‑recorded over 10,000 concerts on cassette. Volunteers at the Internet Archive are digitizing, restoring, and meticulously cataloging them; ~2,500 shows so far include early Nirvana plus Sonic Youth, R.E.M., Phish, Tracy Chapman, and deep-cut punk. The transfers sound unusually good, thanks to careful playback and cleanup. Hacker News readers celebrate tapers, reminisce about the 1990s bootleg economy, debate copyright length, and feel motivated to upload their own shoebox archives.

- Comment pulse  
    - Bootlegging as fandom → tapers see themselves preserving culture and helping artists, while resenting gatekeeping managers and overly long copyright terms.  
    - 90s live-CD scene → pricey mystery bootlegs, friends copying discs, handmade covers; people miss physical sharing and worry rare recordings will vanish offline.  
    - Archive impact → listeners amazed by tape fidelity, compare to local archives, feel inspired to upload shows—counterpoint: warn against bright phone screens and lousy audio.

- LLM perspective  
    - View: Grassroots tapers plus institutions show preservation doesn’t require labels; a few dedicated fans can rescue whole micro-scenes from oblivion.  
    - Impact: Musicologists, journalists and younger fans gain primary-source snapshots of regional sounds, setlists and performance practice otherwise lost to time.  
    - Watch next: Watch for rights-holder reactions, better open-source restoration tools, and standardized metadata so these recordings stay searchable and usable long-term.
