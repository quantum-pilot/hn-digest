# Why has there been so little progress on Alzheimer's disease?

- Score: 406 | [HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47905984) | Link: https://freakonomics.com/podcast/why-has-there-been-so-little-progress-on-alzheimers-disease/

- TL;DR  
    - Alzheimer’s research locked onto the amyloid‑beta hypothesis for decades, helped by influential (possibly fraudulent) image data and powerful funding/publication incentives. This produced many plaque‑clearing drugs with marginal, disputed clinical benefit while other hypotheses and multi‑factor models got starved of support. Commenters emphasize Alzheimer’s likely comprises heterogeneous, multi‑causal diseases; drug development is inherently slow and expensive; and the deeper problem is a research ecosystem prone to groupthink, sunk‑cost lock‑in, and weak mechanisms for course correction.

- Comment pulse  
    - Amyloid as primary cause → Early lab models and genetics looked convincing, locking in careers and grants around plaques even as mechanistic doubts accumulated.  
    - Groupthink and incentives → Reviewers, funders, and journals sidelined non‑amyloid ideas; critics blame a broken science culture—counterpoint: others see sincere error in a hard problem.  
    - Amyloid not dead → Some cite strong genetic links and small benefits from Lecanemab/Donanemab; skeptics note weak symptom change and treatment starting far too late.

- LLM perspective  
    - View: Treat Alzheimer’s as overlapping syndromes; design trials around subtypes, early biomarkers, and multi‑target interventions rather than single magic bullets.  
    - Impact: Shift funding from monolithic programs to diversified bets; reward replication, negative results, and shared tools for imaging, genetics, cohorts.  
    - Watch next: Prevention trials in pre‑symptomatic patients, amyloid vs non‑amyloid drugs, and regulatory reforms on surrogate endpoints and adaptive designs.
