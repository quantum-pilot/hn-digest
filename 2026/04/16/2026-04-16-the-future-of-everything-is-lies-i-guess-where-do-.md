# The future of everything is lies, I guess: Where do we go from here?

- Score: 478 | [HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47792718) | Link: https://aphyr.com/posts/420-the-future-of-everything-is-lies-i-guess-where-do-we-go-from-here

## TL;DR
Kingsbury closes his long LLM critique by shifting from capabilities to societal “city planning.” Like cars, he argues, ML will reshape culture, work, information, and power—with harms already visible: spammy slop, fraud, surveillance, job erosion, and weakened human skills. He urges a personal and collective slowdown: don’t normalize LLMs, unionize against mandated use, regulate carbon and digital harms, and even quit frontier labs, buying time to adapt. Yet he ends conflicted, tempted by narrowly safe, convenience-driven use.

## Comment pulse
- Debate whether AI devalues reading, writing, thinking: some see prior cognitive premium as historical anomaly; others reject regression to primogeniture and physical brutality.  
- Many praise the series and car analogy, yet favor constrained LLM use plus policy, education, and engaged experts over blanket personal refusal.  
- Some foresee AI as oligarchic control tool enabling "useless eater" culling—counterpoint: critics say current systems are brittle automation, best handled via regulation, not revolution.

## LLM perspective
- Treat LLMs as high-friction tools for narrow, inspectable tasks, not default co-pilots for thought or institutions.  
- Channel activism where failures are expensive: workplaces, unions, standards bodies, procurement, insurers, and critical-infrastructure regulators.  
- Watch empirical studies on productivity and skill atrophy, energy-use disclosures, and concrete abuse cases that force regulation and liability precedents.
