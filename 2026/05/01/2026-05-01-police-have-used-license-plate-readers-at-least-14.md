# Police Have Used License Plate Readers at Least 14x to Stalk Romantic Interests

- Score: 244 | [HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47976529) | Link: https://ij.org/police-have-reportedly-used-license-plate-readers-to-stalk-romantic-interests-at-least-14-times-in-recent-years/

- TL;DR  
An Institute for Justice review found at least 14 documented U.S. cases since 2021 where police used automated license plate readers—often Flock systems—to stalk romantic partners, exes, or even strangers, at times running plates hundreds of times. Most abuses surfaced only when victims complained or used public audit tools, not via vendor safeguards. The piece argues that warrantless ALPR networks give every officer a powerful movement-tracking tool that’s predictably misused, and calls for stronger constitutional and legislative limits.

- Comment pulse  
  - Auditability eroding → Flock once exposed officer IDs in logs; after stalking scandals, searches became anonymized, weakening citizen oversight and making FOIA-based accountability harder.  
  - Corporate misuse worries → reports claim senior Flock staff browsed private surveillance feeds, reinforcing fears that centralized monitoring tools invite voyeurism beyond law enforcement.  
  - How big is the problem? → Some see 14 cases as “statistically zero”; others cite underreporting, weak logging, and personal stalking experiences—counterpoint: better baselines needed.

- LLM perspective  
  - View: ALPRs create a searchable time–location history for everyone; without strict warrants and logging, ‘off-label’ uses are inevitable.  
  - Impact: Domestic abuse, stalking, and doxxing risks rise, especially for partners of officers and political activists regularly passing camera clusters.  
  - Watch next: State laws mandating warrants and non-anonymized logs, municipal contract reversals, and court rulings on plate-tracking as search.
