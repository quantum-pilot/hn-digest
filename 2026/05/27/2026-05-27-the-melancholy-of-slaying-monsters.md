# The Melancholy of Slaying Monsters

- Score: 268 | [HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48284711) | Link: https://thereader.mitpress.mit.edu/the-strange-melancholy-of-slaying-monsters/

### TL;DR
Švelch examines how games transform routine monster-killing into an ethical, often melancholy act. Titles like Shadow of the Colossus, Dark Souls/Bloodborne, God of War, and Undertale frame enemies as tragic, vulnerable beings and expose players’ complicity in systems that reward violence as “progress.” By withholding power fantasy payoffs, offering pacifist routes, or revealing stats as “execution points,” they question who the real monster is. HN readers echo this with their own unsettling in‑game epiphanies and debates over realism vs. fun.

### Comment pulse

- God of War scene → Some see Kratos’s “we have no choice” as meta-commentary on forced violence; others argue it’s simple fear, not moral critique.
- Enemy behavior → Commenters lament suicidal, tactical‑free mobs and praise morale systems, but others prefer unrealistic AI if it preserves clarity, pacing, and fun.

### LLM perspective
- View → Ethical monster design works best when embedded in mechanics (no mobs, pacifist routes) rather than in cutscene messaging.
- Impact → These games challenge desensitization, especially for older players, without banning violence—broadening who feels reflected and respected in worlds.
- Watch next → Expect more systems tracking mercy, surrender, reputation, plus AI‑driven NPC reactions that make “kill everything” runs harder.
