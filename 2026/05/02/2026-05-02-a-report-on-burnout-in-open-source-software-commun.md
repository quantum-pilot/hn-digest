# A report on burnout in open source software communities (2025) [pdf]

- Score: 130 | [HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47981669) | Link: https://mirandaheath.website/static/oss_burnout_report_mh_25.pdf

## TL;DR

The report argues that burnout in open-source software is widespread and structurally driven, not just a matter of individual resilience. Using literature, community content, and interviews, it identifies six reinforcing causes: lack of payment, unsustainable workload, unrewarding maintenance, toxic/entitled user behavior, hyper-responsibility, and pressure to prove oneself (portfolio, identity, gamified metrics). It recommends systemic fixes: reliable and autonomy-preserving funding, cultural norms of respect and gratitude, broader and better-trained contributor communities, and strong advocacy around platforms and policy, especially security regulation and maintainer liability.

## Comment pulse

- OSS is heterogeneous → most projects are tiny, low-pressure hobbies; burnout dynamics mainly hit widely used or critical infrastructure projects—counterpoint: those are exactly the ones everything depends on.  
- Toxic entitlement is severe → maintainers report harassment, boundary violations, even offline fallout; “free” work often attracts disrespect and high-maintenance users.  
- “Just quit if it’s a hobby” vs reality → OSS doubles as portfolio and identity; broken hiring and reputation systems trap people in unpaid, stressful maintenance.  

## LLM perspective

- View: Treat popular OSS like critical infrastructure with service expectations, not as casual side projects; governance and funding should match that reality.  
- Impact: Most urgent for solo maintainers of widely deployed libraries and marginalized contributors facing compounded stressors and harassment.  
- Watch next: Concrete trials of maintainer salaries, decentralized funding, GitHub UX for expectation-setting, AI-contribution filters, and legal carve-outs limiting maintainer security liability.
