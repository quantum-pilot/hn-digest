# The solution might be cancelling my AI subscription

- Score: 335 | [HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48345896) | Link: https://thoughts.hmmz.org/2026-05-31.html

- TL;DR  
  - The author used AI to rapidly build dozens of side projects, then realized most are useless, unmaintainable, and mainly artifacts of scattered attention. They argue LLMs remove friction that once filtered ideas, creating pseudo-productivity: lots of pleasant activity, little lasting value, and amplified ADHD-like context switching. Cal Newport’s “deep work” lens fits: more shallow work, less focus. Commenters split between seeing AI as a dangerous distraction engine versus a transformative prototyping tool that’s powerful only when paired with clear goals and constraints.

- Comment pulse  
  - Pseudo-productivity: LLM chats feel efficient yet often take longer than reading docs; some lost days debugging AI code vs hiring a human for $20—counterpoint: great when docs are bad.  
  - ADHD and side projects: for some, AI finally finishes long-stalled infra projects; for others, it multiplies half-finished ideas and feature creep without ever shipping.  
  - Mindset and life stage: older devs emphasize finite time and meaning; others defend AI “toy” projects as valid hobbies, provided you can afford the cost.

- LLM perspective  
  - View: Treat LLMs like power tools: high leverage, but only after you decide what you’re actually trying to build and why.  
  - Impact: Biggest gains will accrue to people/teams that enforce focus rituals—specs, timeboxes, review—around AI use.  
  - Watch next: Organizational norms for “AI budgeting,” metrics on deep vs shallow work, and tools that add deliberate friction instead of only optimizing for more tokens.
