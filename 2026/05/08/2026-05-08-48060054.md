# Ask HN: We just had an actual UUID v4 collision...

- Score: 286 | [HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48060054) | Link: https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48060054

- TL;DR  
  A UUIDv4 collision shocked the OP, but commenters note real collisions usually expose bad randomness: weak entropy, buggy libraries, or browser/VM quirks, not math failure. High‑assurance systems often forbid UUIDv4 and carefully engineer and monitor entropy, while some companies hilariously overreact with central “UUID services” and databases of all IDs. Comparisons to SHA‑1 and shuffled decks underline how vanishingly small collisions are with proper RNGs. Main advice: treat collisions as RNG incidents, validate randomness implementations, and design systems to cope gracefully with occasional duplicates.

- Comment pulse  
  - Collisions usually indicate broken randomness: low entropy, unseeded PRNGs, VM/fork duplication, buggy libs; serious systems avoid v4 and invest heavily in monitored entropy sources.  
  - Generating UUIDs in browsers is risky: poor RNGs, bots with deterministic crypto.getRandomValues, and deliberate collisions; better to generate IDs on trusted backends.  
  - Some teams built central UUID microservices tracking every ID in databases, while others note SHA‑1/Git analogies showing collision odds are extremely low with correct randomness.

- LLM perspective  
  - View: Treat UUIDv4 as a consumer of cryptographic randomness; engineering focus should be on RNG design, seeding, monitoring, and collision-handling paths.  
  - Impact: Backend, infra, and browser vendors need stronger guarantees around crypto RNG APIs, especially under VMs, containers, sandboxes, bots, and low-entropy boot conditions.  
  - Watch next: Wider adoption of UUIDv7/snowflake-style IDs, standardized RNG health checks, and documented guidance against front-end ID generation for critical uniqueness.
