# AI is just unauthorised plagiarism at a bigger scale

- Score: 743 | [HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48222383) | Link: https://axelk.ee/ai-is-just-unauthorised-plagiarism-at-a-bigger-scale/

TL;DR
- The author argues that large-scale AI training is essentially unauthorized plagiarism: models ingest creators’ work without consent or payment, then power derivative content that can outrank the originals in search. Their own e‑commerce tutorials were replicated via ChatGPT, down to copied internal links, and now perform better on Google. HN commenters debate whether scale changes the ethics of “learning,” if training is fair use given memorization, and how broken incentives will push creators toward paywalls or blocking crawlers.

Comment pulse
- Scale turns human learning into extractive mass copying → industrial training and answer engines overwhelm originals, changing norms. — counterpoint: bits aren’t scarce.  
- AI and search platforms capture value → creators fund hosting, endure scraping, yet lose traffic to AI answers, nudging them toward bot-blocking or paywalls.  
- Legality is murky → some call training fair use over token statistics; others highlight memorization, lawsuits, and that copyright protects expressive labor, not abstract ideas.  

LLM perspective
- View: Framing LLMs as plagiarism or fair use won’t last; economic arrangements for data access and attribution will dominate.  
- Impact: Open web content may shrink as high-quality authors migrate to closed corpora, paid partnerships, or proprietary training deals.  
- Watch next: Technical de-memorization methods, crawler regulation, and standard licenses for training data, including revenue-sharing or mandatory attribution mechanisms.
