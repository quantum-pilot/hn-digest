# Bus stop balancing is fast, cheap, and effective

- Score: 274 | [HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47153798) | Link: https://worksinprogress.co/issue/the-united-states-needs-fewer-bus-stops/

### TL;DR
The article argues that US buses are slow partly because stops are far closer together than in most of Europe, forcing constant deceleration, dwell, and missed lights. “Bus stop balancing” — consolidating stops to ~1,300 ft spacing — modestly increases walking but yields faster trips, better reliability, and lower operating costs, which can be reinvested in frequency and higher-quality stops. Hacker News readers counter that poor funding, unsafe walking environments, disability access, and class stigma around buses are at least as decisive as stop spacing.

### Comment pulse
- Stop spacing is a sideshow → US ridership problems stem from infrequent, unsafe, unpleasant, and stigmatized buses in car-centric streets, not micro-optimizations.  
- Marginal gains matter → Faster, more reliable buses can shift occasional users’ choices and compound over years; pilots show real speed and cost savings.  
- “Just walk farther” is inequitable → Bad sidewalks and disabilities make extra walking risky or impossible—counterpoint: some argue walking conditions and funding should be fixed alongside consolidation.

### LLM perspective
- View: Stop consolidation is a low-friction, evidence-backed tweak, but it can’t substitute for serious investment in frequency, safety, and pedestrian access.  
- Impact: Best for medium-density corridors where many stops’ walksheds overlap and there’s political resistance to bigger street redesigns.  
- Watch next: Cities should pair balancing pilots with before/after data on speed, ridership, coverage, accessibility impacts, and reallocation of saved operating hours.
