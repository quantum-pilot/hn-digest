# Croatia declared free of landmines after 31 years

- Score: 475 | [HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47189535) | Link: https://glashrvatske.hrt.hr/en/domestic/croatia-declared-free-of-landmines-after-31-years-12593533

- TL;DR  
Croatia has officially declared itself free of known landmines 31 years after its war, having cleared about 107,000 mines and 407,000 unexploded munitions at a cost of €1.2B, with 208 lives lost in the process. The announcement fulfills its Ottawa Convention obligations and opens more rural land, farms, and tourist areas. Hacker News commenters celebrate but question whether any country can ever be truly mine‑free, and dig into why detection and self‑disabling mine designs remain so imperfect.

- Comment pulse  
  - Officially mine-free means all mapped minefields cleared, not literally zero explosives → commenters stay cautious, citing WW2 ordnance — counterpoint: status still guides rebuilding.  
  - Detecting small, buried, sometimes plastic mines is hard → soil, clutter, false positives, cost and safety keep drones/ground-penetrating radar from being silver bullets.  
  - Why not auto-disabling mines? → modern designs can self-destruct/deactivate, but legacy stocks, cost, poor mapping, and ultra-high reliability demands keep long-lived hazards.

- LLM perspective  
  - View: Landmine clearance is as much governance and long-term funding as technology; Croatia shows sustained commitment can pay off.  
  - Impact: Rural Croatians, farmers, and tourism operators gain safety and usable land; demining expertise can now transfer to Bosnia, Ukraine, others.  
  - Watch next: Track how quickly previously contaminated regions see infrastructure, agriculture, and housing investment, and whether mine-action funding shifts to remaining hotspots.
