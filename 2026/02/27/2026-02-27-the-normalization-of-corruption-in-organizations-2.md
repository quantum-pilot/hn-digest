# The normalization of corruption in organizations (2003) [pdf]

- Score: 255 | [HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47177186) | Link: https://gwern.net/doc/sociology/2003-ashforth.pdf

### TL;DR
The paper explains how serious corporate misbehavior becomes “normal” inside organizations via three reinforcing processes. First, *institutionalization*: a one‑off shady decision, often enabled by permissive leaders, becomes embedded in routines, structures, and culture, then routinized and mindless. Second, *rationalization*: shared stories (no one’s hurt; everyone does it; it’s technically legal; higher loyalty to the firm) let people see themselves as good. Third, *socialization*: newcomers are inducted into these practices. HN readers connect this to ingroup loyalty, everyday norm-breaking like traffic violations, and large-scale political and cultural corruption.

- Slippery normalization → Small, “justified” rule-bending (from traffic behavior to workplace corners cut) escalates as discomfort fades and dissenters leave, producing brazen but culturally accepted misconduct.  
- Institutions and culture → Weak legal/symbolic sanctions (India’s petty bribery, US “thank-you” gifts) entrench corruption, while universalist cultures (e.g., Singapore anecdotes, some New England history) can suppress it.
