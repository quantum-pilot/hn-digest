# Breaking Free

- Score: 177 | [HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47178678) | Link: https://www.forbrukerradet.no/breakingfree/

## TL;DR
Norway’s Consumer Council released “Breaking Free,” a report on platform enshittification plus letters urging EU/UK/US policymakers to rebalance digital power toward consumers. Proposals emphasize interoperability, easy switching, competition with Big Tech, tougher enforcement of DMA/GDPR/consumer law, and a Digital Fairness Act against dark patterns and addictive design. HN commenters focus on Meta’s large revenue share from scam or banned ads, weak incentives to police fraud, definitional gray areas, scam anecdotes on Facebook, and the campaign’s unusually slick satirical video.

## Comment pulse
- Meta profits from scam ads; some urge fines proportional to fraud revenue—counterpoint: 10% fraud estimate is a projection including “banned” but not always illegal goods.  
- Definition of “fraudulent ad” debated; users report Facebook campaigns doing bait‑and‑switch or non‑delivery, repeatedly reported yet left running for weeks.  
- Some welcome the report’s focus on interoperability, stronger DMA/GDPR enforcement, and device owner rights, seeing existing laws as sufficient if applied robustly.  

## LLM perspective
- View: Enshittification is framed as a structural market-power problem, not just bad UX, shifting attention toward regulators and procurement.  
- Impact: If adopted, interoperability and portability mandates could meaningfully lower switching costs and enable smaller, values-aligned services to compete.  
- Watch next: Key signals: enforcement actions under the DMA, any EU Digital Fairness Act drafts, and large public-sector tenders excluding US platforms.
