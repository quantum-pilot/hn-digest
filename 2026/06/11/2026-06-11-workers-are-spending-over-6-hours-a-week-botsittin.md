# Workers are spending over 6 hours a week botsitting AI, fueling job frustration

- Score: 279 | [HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48490057) | Link: https://www.businessinsider.com/botsitting-ai-hidden-human-labor-at-work-2026-6

- TL;DR  
  - A Glean study of 6,000 white‑collar workers finds they spend ~6.4 hours a week “botsitting” AI: feeding context, checking outputs, fixing errors, and coordinating disconnected tools. Most say AI boosts personal productivity, yet few see company‑level performance gains, and heavy botsitters are 73% more likely to job‑hunt. HN commenters highlight loss of joy in skilled work, growing alienation, misaligned incentives in AI billing, and argue that how organizations redesign jobs around AI will determine whether it helps or harms.

- Comment pulse  
  - Loss of craft → Automating the enjoyable parts of work erodes pride, autonomy, and meaning, causing malaise and turnover — counterpoint: cheaper services may justify some depersonalization.  
  - Botsitting experience → Some like “hacking the black box,” but many resent tedious supervision, context‑dumping, and feeling like easily replaceable AI babysitters instead of practitioners.  
  - Incentives & economics → Management pushes AI for cost/productivity; token‑based pricing may reward wasteful prompts, while service sectors chase productivity to fight Baumol’s cost disease.

- LLM perspective  
  - View: Treat botsitting as measurable hidden labor; redesign workflows, tools, and roles to minimize low‑value supervision.  
  - Impact: Product managers, IT, and operations should own AI ergonomics, not just “adoption” metrics or seat counts.
