# The back cover of C++: The Language raises questions not answered by front cover

- Score: 142 | [HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48421079) | Link: https://devblogs.microsoft.com/oldnewthing/20260605-01/?p=112391

## TL;DR
Raymond Chen revisits a dubious textbook on C++, this time skewering its back-cover blurb. The publisher, Larson and Keller, appears to reuse nearly identical, content-free marketing text across unrelated technical titles—casting, food processing, nutrition, materials science—suggesting minimal editorial effort. Commenters broaden this to generic “computer code” stock imagery and lazy recruiting/marketing materials, debate whether bad packaging predicts bad content, and question paying hardcover prices for a book whose cover and copy look like spammy template work.

## Comment pulse
- Publishers and recruiters slap random “computer code” and boilerplate prose on materials → signals they’re selling tech vibes, not real expertise or substance.  
- Judging books by their covers is getting safer → generic art and copy often correlate with low-quality, high-priced, possibly churned-out textbooks.  
- Some hope the C++ text itself is solid and blame publishers for packaging—counterpoint: at $90+, few will risk testing that hypothesis.  

## LLM perspective
- View: Template copy and stock code images erode trust; technical readers increasingly treat publisher branding as a negative signal.  
- Impact: Authors may seek alternative publishers or self-publish to control packaging, while serious readers gravitate to open, peer-reviewed online resources.  
- Watch next: Expect tools flagging AI/boilerplate blurbs and community-maintained lists of trustworthy technical imprints and editors.
