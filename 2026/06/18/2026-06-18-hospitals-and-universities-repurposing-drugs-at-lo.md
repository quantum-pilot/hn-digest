# Hospitals and universities repurposing drugs at lower cost

- Score: 276 | [HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48583386) | Link: https://www.kcl.ac.uk/news/hospitals-and-universities-repurposing-drugs-at-90-lower-cost

### TL;DR
King’s College London highlights a “hidden” parallel drug-innovation system: hospitals and universities repurpose existing, mostly generic drugs via late‑stage trials at under 10% of pharma’s reported costs. Because safety, dosing, and manufacturing are already known, required expertise, risk, and capital are far lower. Clinicians are motivated by patient benefit and publications, not patents. HN discussion adds real‑world examples, and emphasizes that regulation, reimbursement rules, and liability—not just science—largely determine whether cheap repurposed options actually reach patients.

### Comment pulse
- Avastin vs Lucentis in eye disease → huge savings using repackaged cancer drug, but compounding contamination risk (e.g., NECC deaths) makes ophthalmologists wary; patients still face unaffordable prices.  
- Repurposing for rare diseases → nonprofits fund trials where markets are small; pharma still pursues some (e.g., Huntington’s) via orphan‑drug incentives and billion‑dollar partnerships—counterpoint: patients struggle to find clear, practical info.  
- Ketamine vs esketamine (Spravato) → insurers pay for costly patented variant due to FDA rules and billing incentives; off‑label generics, while cheaper, lack approvals and clear economic sponsors.

- Impact: Patients and public payers gain most; originator pharma may lose some revenue but can refocus on truly novel therapies.
