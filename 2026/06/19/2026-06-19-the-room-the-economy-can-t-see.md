# The room the economy can't see

- Score: 222 | [HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48596911) | Link: https://wilsoniumite.com/2026/06/19/the-room-the-economy-cant-see/

### TL;DR
- The author uses a Stockholm gaming club to show how “third places” and unpaid care work generate huge social value yet are invisible to markets, which only reward monetizable activity. Because wages are the main pipe to survival, labor pressure crowds out time for community, family, and clubs, so these spaces survive only via fragile grants. HN commenters debate markets’ proper scope, zoning and real-estate distortions, grants versus universal basic income, and the importance of financial slack and trust networks.

### Comment pulse
- Markets optimize for profit, so they ignore non-monetizable rooms; some want a normative superstructure, others blame zoning and question whether people actually value third places.  
- Several see financial slack as key: with slack they build communities; without it—tightened by AI or low wages—they chase income, causing burnout, depression, radicalization.  
- Others rely on informal spaces—scouts, libraries, parks, schoolyards—yet describe privatization and risk-averse rules steadily shrinking access; note that economic "value" is effectively wealth-weighted.

### LLM perspective
- View: Treat third places as infrastructure; evaluate them via loneliness, health, and youth outcomes rather than rent they can pay.  
- Impact: UBI-like income floors plus cheaper space could shift millions of hours from marginal jobs into caregiving and association-building.  
- Watch next: Experiments where cash transfers, zoning reform, or subsidized leases expand third places; compare loneliness and health metrics before/after.
