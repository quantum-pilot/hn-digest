# Mercedes‑Benz starts large‑scale production of electric axial flux motor

- Score: 549 | [HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48472877) | Link: https://media.mercedes-benz.com/en/article/bebac2af-acdc-465a-9538-adb0bf3d8ccf

- TL;DR  
  - Mercedes‑Benz has begun large‑scale production of axial flux electric motors at its historic Berlin‑Marienfelde plant, turning it into a high‑performance EV motor hub and Digital Factory Campus. The line uses 98 tightly controlled steps, new laser processes, AI‑based quality control and 30+ patentable innovations to industrialize a design from its UK subsidiary YASA. These ultra‑compact motors debut in the all‑electric AMG GT 4‑door coupé, enabling 0–100 km/h in 2.1 s, and are framed politically as a win for German and European industry. Hacker News discussion focuses on what axial flux motors are, how they differ from conventional EV motors, and whether they’ll remain a performance niche or become mainstream.

- Comment pulse  
  - Axial flux appeal → commenters highlight compact, powerful home‑lab builds and agile robotics (quadrupeds climbing stairs with heavy loads) enabled by these thin, disk‑like motors.  
  - Adoption outlook → many expect radial motors to dominate mass market due to proven reliability; axial seen as premium/performance tech for years—counterpoint: thinner designs might eventually cut materials and cost.  
  - Industrial context → electric motors’ simpler factories versus ICE are cited as a reason China advanced quickly; some note YASA’s earlier HN thread drew little interest before Mercedes’ acquisition.

- LLM perspective  
  - View: The real breakthrough is not the motor concept, but proving repeatable, high‑yield manufacturing of a previously “lab‑only” architecture.  
  - Impact: Short term, high‑end EVs and motorsport; longer term, lessons in tolerances, automation, and QA could spill into robotics and micromobility.  
  - Watch next: Field reliability data, cost per kW versus radial designs, and whether smaller axial units appear in ebikes or next‑gen Formula E.
