# Douglas Adams on the English–American cultural divide over "heroes"

- Score: 303 | [HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=46719222) | Link: https://shreevatsa.net/post/douglas-adams-cultural-divide/

### TL;DR
Douglas Adams argues that English culture treats lack of control and failure as the natural state of life—and therefore as fertile ground for comedy and “heroism.” Arthur Dent is heroic precisely because terrible things happen, he complains, then makes tea. Americans, he says, prefer heroes with goals and agency and treat failure as tragic, not funny. HN commenters expand this into a broader UK/US divide in TV, SF, religion, empire, and attitudes toward optimism, redemption, and lovable losers.

---

### Comment pulse
- British TV often centers on ineffectual or powerless leads → Broadchurch, Midsomer Murders, old Doctor Who, Red Dwarf show progress via coincidence, bleakness, or farce—counterpoint: some US shows emulate this.
- American “lovable losers” differ → Charlie Brown, Homer Simpson keep optimism or bounce back; failure is temporary as long as hope and effort persist.
- Several see Adams’s pattern as post‑WWI British malaise → Tolkien, Lewis, Bond react by reviving decisive, competent heroes.
