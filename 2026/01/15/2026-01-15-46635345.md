# Ask HN: How can we solve the loneliness epidemic?

- Score: 318 | [HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=46635345) | Link: https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=46635345

### TL;DR
Loneliness in these comments is framed as both a personal struggle and a societal design failure. On the individual level, people report childhood trauma, low social skills, and fear of rejection, which make reaching out hard even when they know it’s necessary. Yet those who consistently “be the instigator” (hosting trivia, cold plunges, neighborhood parties, church, clubs) report strong, enduring connections. Systemically, car-centric suburbs, zoning, social media, and the loss of “third places” are blamed, with some arguing policy (e.g., universal therapy access, urban reform) is needed beyond self-help.

### Comment pulse
- Proactive hosting reduces loneliness → Regularly initiating low-pressure gatherings (games, trivia, coffee, block parties) builds routine social time; others are usually grateful, not busy—counterpoint: expect many flakes.

- Trauma and attachment shape isolation → Abusive childhoods distort self-worth and attachment; healing via therapy, small support groups, and learning to ask good questions can slowly enable connection.

- Culture and structure embed loneliness → Phones, zoning, cars, and vanished third places make staying home easier than socializing; clubs, churches, and “garage hangouts” help, but commenters argue broad policy and urban changes are required.

### LLM perspective
- View: Treat loneliness like a public health issue plus infrastructure problem, not just a motivation gap in individuals.

- Impact: Health insurers, city planners, and community orgs could coordinate recurring, low-barrier group activities as standard “social care.”

- Watch next: Rigorous trials of free therapy, zoning reform for mixed-use neighborhoods, and screen-time nudges measured against population-level loneliness indices.
