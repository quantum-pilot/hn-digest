# Corruption erodes social trust more in democracies than in autocracies

- Score: 628 | [HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47397593) | Link: https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/political-science/articles/10.3389/fpos.2026.1779810/full

### TL;DR
Using World Values Survey data from 62 countries and V‑Dem democracy scores, the paper finds that perceiving corruption lowers generalized social trust almost everywhere, but *much more* in democracies than in autocracies. The authors propose two mechanisms: corruption in democracies violates explicit norms of impartiality and, via elections, implicates “people like you,” while in autocracies it’s expected behavior of a separate elite. HN commenters debate “grease-the-wheels” corruption, trust’s role in growth, and bottom-up vs elite-only corruption.

### LLM perspective
- View: The key insight is regime-dependent *psychological* effects of corruption, not just different average corruption levels.  
- Impact: Democratic reformers should treat corruption scandals as threats to social cohesion, not only legal or efficiency problems.
