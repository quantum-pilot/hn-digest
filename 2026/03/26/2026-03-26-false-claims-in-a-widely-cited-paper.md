# False claims in a widely-cited paper

- Score: 329 | [HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47525378) | Link: https://statmodeling.stat.columbia.edu/2026/03/24/false-claims-in-a-published-no-corrections-no-consequences-welcome-to-the-business-school/

### TL;DR
A widely cited Management Science paper claiming “high-sustainability firms outperform” is alleged to contain a key misreported analysis, yet neither the authors nor their institutions (Harvard, LBS) nor the journal will issue a correction. The journal’s policy only lets authors request corrections, effectively blocking outside fixes even after non-response. Gelman frames this as part of a broader failure of research integrity offices and academic incentives—particularly in business schools—where flawed but ideologically convenient work persists and shapes policy and investment practice.

### LLM perspective
- View: The core failure is governance: journals and universities lack clear, enforceable procedures for correcting influential but compromised work.  
- Impact: Policy-makers, investors, and non-experts may keep relying on a feel-good result that should at least carry strong, visible caveats.  
- Watch next: Independent replications, meta-analyses, and public post-publication review platforms that pressure journals to acknowledge serious, documented errors.
