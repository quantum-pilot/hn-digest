# Supreme Court Sides with Cox in Copyright Fight over Pirated Music

- Score: 247 | [HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47518281) | Link: https://www.nytimes.com/2026/03/25/us/politics/supreme-court-cox-music-copyright.html

- TL;DR  
  - The Supreme Court unanimously held that Cox isn’t contributorily liable for subscribers’ music piracy, ruling that ISPs aren’t responsible merely for knowing infringement occurs or for lax termination policies; they must intend or encourage infringement to be on the hook. The decision, rooted in the Sony Betamax precedent, relieves ISPs from acting as copyright police, while HN commenters debate its significance for surveillance, safe-harbor rules, and broader reforms to an increasingly controversial copyright system.

- Comment pulse  
  - Case context → SCOTUS reversed a Fourth Circuit ruling that found Cox contributorily liable; reiterated Sony Betamax: tools/services with substantial lawful uses aren't automatically infringing.  
  - Open-internet view → Decision averts billion‑dollar ISP liability and mass disconnections based on unproven notices, reducing incentives for deep traffic monitoring—counterpoint: rightsholders lose an enforcement lever.  
  - Copyright policy angst → Commenters argue current terms are long, propose tiered renewals, and highlight contradictions between piracy penalties and permissive AI training on copyrighted data.

- LLM perspective  
  - View: The ruling narrows contributory infringement, reinforcing that general‑purpose infrastructure isn’t liable absent clear inducement or targeted infringement features.  
  - Impact: ISPs, clouds, CDNs, and possibly AI hosting providers gain legal cover, but must still maintain documented repeat‑infringer policies.  
  - Watch next: Lobbying to tighten DMCA safe harbors, cases testing where “intent” begins for ad‑supported piracy services, and AI‑copyright litigation.
