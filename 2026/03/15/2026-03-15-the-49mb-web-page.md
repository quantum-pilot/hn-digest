# The 49MB web page

- Score: 232 | [HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47390945) | Link: https://thatshubham.com/blog/news-audit

### TL;DR
An engineer profiles a New York Times article that needed 422 requests and 49 MB, arguing modern news sites have become hostile, surveillance-heavy apps instead of documents. He shows how client-side ad auctions, trackers, modals, sticky videos, and layout-shifting ads burn CPU, battery, and attention, all to goose ad metrics like viewability and time-on-page. He suggests design and engineering fixes—delayed/serialized overlays, reserved ad slots, lighter variants, RSS—and urges users to abandon abusive sites, while HN debates practicality and economics.

### Comment pulse
- Bloat often stems from un-throttled dev environments: fast fiber, beefy machines, preloaded videos. People recommend bandwidth/CPU throttling and slow staging servers to expose pain.  
- Some users block JS, use text.npr.org or lite.cnn.com, and never see ads—counterpoint: they’re a tiny, non-paying minority news sites ignore.  
- Commenters connect page obesity to failing journalism economics: print-era ad subsidies vanished, so outlets chase surveillance-based ad targeting and dark patterns to survive, despite hypocrisy.

### LLM perspective
- View: Web news UX problems are incentive-driven; technical fixes matter, but business metrics must change to reward reader satisfaction.  
- Impact: Users with blockers already opt out; real leverage comes from regulators, app-store rules, and browser defaults pushing against abusive patterns.  
- Watch next: Track adoption of lightweight “text” frontends, ad-tech reforms, and Core Web Vitals enforcement on large publishers’ rankings and revenue.
