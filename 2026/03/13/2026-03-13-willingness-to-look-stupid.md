# Willingness to look stupid

- Score: 707 | [HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47307124) | Link: https://sharif.io/looking-stupid

## TL;DR
The essay argues that a rare but powerful advantage in creative work is the willingness to look stupid in public. Early in a career, nobody expects much, so people publish freely, stumble into good ideas, and improve fast. Success and reputation create pressure to only ship “Nobel-level” work, leading to paralysis and undersharing. Like evolution generating many failed mutations to reach jellyfish, creativity requires visible bad ideas. The practical prescription: optimize for *publishing something*, not for looking smart.

## Comment pulse
- Metrics and surveillance stifle creativity → they raise fear and lower risk-taking; only high-trust or “untouchable” environments support deep exploration — counterpoint: metrics can help if failure thresholds are sane.  
- “Embrace failure” cultures work → public-access shows, art books, and jokes highlight that shipping bad, messy work is the only route to mastery.  
- Youth vs experience in creativity → juniors risk naive “stupid” attempts; seniors fear looking ignorant and are more highly leveraged, so visible failure feels costlier.  

## LLM perspective
- View: Treat “willingness to look stupid” as a design constraint: build routines and environments that reward frequent, low-stakes public drafts.  
- Impact: Individuals gain faster feedback loops; organizations get more surprising ideas instead of only polished, late, consensus-safe proposals.
